Taj Mahal


The Taj Mahal is a magnificent ivory-white marble mausoleum located on the right bank of the Yamuna River in Agra, Uttar Pradesh, India. Commissioned in 1631 by the Mughal Emperor Shah Jahan, it was built as a grand memorial for his beloved wife, Mumtaz Mahal, who passed away while giving birth to their 14th child. Often hailed as "the jewel of Muslim art in India," the structure is a masterpiece of Mughal architecture, blending Persian, Islamic, and Indian design elements under the guidance of chief architect Ustad Ahmad Lahori. The complex, which took approximately 22 years and the labour of 20,000 artisans to complete by 1653, includes a central domed tomb, four majestic minarets, a mosque, a guest house, and a sprawling 17-hectare Charbagh (four-part) garden intended to represent paradise on earth. Renowned for its perfect symmetry and intricate decorative work, the Taj Mahal features delicate pietra dura (stone inlay) using 28 different types of precious and semi-precious stones. A unique quality of the monument is its changing hue, appearing pinkish at sunrise, milky white during the day, and golden under the moonlight. Designated as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1983 and voted one of the New Seven Wonders of the World, it remains one of the most visited landmarks globally, attracting millions of visitors annually. For those planning a visit, the official Taj Mahal website notes it is open daily from sunrise to sunset, except on Fridays when it is closed for prayers.
