Colosseum


The Colosseum, originally known as the Flavian Amphitheatre, is the largest ancient amphitheatre ever built and remains the ultimate symbol of Imperial Rome’s architectural and engineering prowess. Commissioned by Emperor Vespasian around 72 AD and inaugurated by his son Titus in 80 AD, this massive freestanding structure was constructed of travertine limestone, volcanic tuff, and concrete, capable of holding between 50,000 and 80,000 spectators. It primarily hosted brutal gladiator contests, animal hunts, and public executions, and was even designed with a sophisticated retractable awning called the velarium to shade the audience. Beneath the arena floor lay the hypogeum, a complex network of tunnels and lifts used to dramatically release gladiators and wild animals through trapdoors. Despite suffering from earthquakes, fires, and centuries of being used as a stone quarry, the Colosseum was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1980 and continues to attract over 7 million visitors annually as one of the New Seven Wonders of the World.
