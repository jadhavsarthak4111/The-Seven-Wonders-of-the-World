The Great Wall of China


The Great Wall of China is a massive system of fortifications, watchtowers, and natural barriers stretching over 21,196 kilometres (13,171 miles) across northern China. Originally conceived by Emperor Qin Shi Huang in the 3rd century BCE to protect the empire from northern nomadic invasions, it was not built as a single continuous structure but as a series of walls joined and expanded over nearly 2,000 years by multiple dynasties. The most famous and best-preserved sections, such as Badaling and Mutianyu, were constructed during the Ming Dynasty (1368–1644) using durable stone and brick held together by a unique sticky rice mortar. Beyond its military role, the wall served as a border control for Silk Road trade and remains a powerful symbol of Chinese resilience and architectural ingenuity. In 1987, it was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site, and it continues to attract millions of visitors as one of the New Seven Wonders of the World.
